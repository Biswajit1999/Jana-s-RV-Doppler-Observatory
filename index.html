Core EPRV physics

The mathematical layer behind radial-velocity exoplanet detection.

Doppler shift

Small wavelength shifts reveal the line-of-sight reflex motion of the host star.

Δλ / λ = vᵣ / c

RV semi-amplitude

The observable amplitude depends on planet mass, stellar mass, inclination, period, and eccentricity.

K = (2πG/P)¹ᐟ³ · (mₚ sin i)/(mₛ + mₚ)²ᐟ³ · 1/√(1 − e²)

Mass function

RV usually gives a minimum mass because inclination is unknown.

f(m) = P K³(1 − e²)³ᐟ² / 2πG

Kepler’s third law

Links orbital period and total system mass to the orbital semi-major axis.

P² = 4π²a³ / G(mₛ + mₚ)

Keplerian RV model

Used for first-pass model curves and observed-minus-computed residuals.

RV(t) = K[cos(ω + ν(t)) + e cosω] + γ

Earth-twin scale

An Earth analogue around a Sun-like star produces a signal of only about 9–10 cm/s.

K⊕ ≈ 0.09 m/s

Cross-match RV time-series, spectra, CCF indicators, photometry and astrometry around each target.

Analyse spectral lines independently because different lines respond differently to spots and convection.

3. Clean wobble estimate

Use activity-sensitive channels to estimate stellar jitter before comparing residuals with planet models.
